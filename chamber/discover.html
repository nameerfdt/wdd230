History

Folsom's history is closely tied to the California Gold Rush. In 1848, gold was discovered in nearby Coloma, sparking the gold rush that brought thousands of fortune seekers to the region. Folsom's location along the American River made it an important hub for gold miners and prospectors.

Folsom was officially founded in 1855 and named after Joseph Libbey Folsom, who was one of the directors of the company that purchased the land where the town was established. Folsom quickly grew as a center of commerce and transportation due to its location on the Sacramento Valley Railroad.

One of the most well-known landmarks in Folsom is the Folsom State Prison, which was established in 1880. It gained notoriety for the song "Folsom Prison Blues" by Johnny Cash, who performed at the prison in 1968.

Folsom became one of the first cities in the United States to have a hydroelectric power plant in 1895. The Folsom Powerhouse, operated by the Sacramento Municipal Utility District (SMUD), played a crucial role in providing electricity to the region.

Folsom served as a vital transportation hub during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, thanks to its location along the Sacramento Valley Railroad. The railroad facilitated the transportation of goods and people to and from Folsom, contributing to its economic growth.

The Folsom Dam, completed in 1955, was constructed by the United States Army Corps of Engineers to control flooding along the American River. The reservoir created by the dam, Folsom Lake, is a popular recreational area today.

In recent decades, Folsom has experienced significant growth and development, becoming a thriving suburb of Sacramento. The city is known for its high quality of life, excellent schools, and recreational amenities.

Recreational Activities

Folsom is Distinctive by Nature. Enjoy more than 50 miles of paved recreational trails, Folsom Lake, Lake Natoma, and the serene American River. Mountatin biking, hiking, and paddleboarding are just a few of the popular activities of Folsom.

: Folsom Lake State Recreation Area is a popular destination for outdoor enthusiasts. Visitors can enjoy boating, fishing, swimming, hiking, picnicking, and camping along the shores of Folsom Lake. The park also features scenic trails for hiking, mountain biking, and horseback riding.

: The American River Parkway runs through Folsom and offers opportunities for walking, jogging, cycling, and horseback riding along the scenic American River. The parkway features paved trails, natural areas, and picnic areas with beautiful views of the river and surrounding landscape.

: Historic Old Folsom is a charming district with historic buildings, shops, restaurants, and galleries. Visitors can stroll along Sutter Street, explore the Folsom History Museum, visit the Railroad Block, or take a guided walking tour to learn about the city's history.

: Folsom has several golf courses where enthusiasts can enjoy a round of golf surrounded by scenic landscapes. Courses such as Empire Ranch Golf Club and Folsom Lake Golf Course offer challenging holes and beautiful views of the surrounding area.

Surrounding Attractions

Take advantage of Folsom's relatively close proximity to various California attractions for a quick day trip and weekend get away.

: 75 miles northeast. Lake Tahoe is renowned for its breathtaking natural beauty, with crystal-clear blue waters surrounded by majestic snow-capped peaks and lush forests. The scenic vistas attract visitors year-round for outdoor recreation and relaxation.The lake offers a plethora of outdoor activities, including boating, kayaking, paddleboarding, fishing, and swimming in the summer months. In winter, the surrounding ski resorts, including Heavenly, Squaw Valley, and Northstar, draw crowds for skiing, snowboarding, and other snow sports.

: 150 miles southeast.Yosemite is renowned for its stunning natural beauty, characterized by towering granite cliffs, dramatic waterfalls, pristine alpine meadows, and ancient sequoia groves. The park's diverse landscapes offer breathtaking vistas around every corner. Yosemite features several iconic landmarks, including El Capitan, a massive granite monolith popular with rock climbers; Half Dome, a distinctive granite dome with a sheer face rising over the valley; and Yosemite Falls, one of the tallest waterfalls in North America.

: 160 miles northeast. Lassen Volcanic National Park is home to a wide range of volcanic features, including lava beds, cinder cones, hot springs, fumaroles, and mud pots. Visitors can explore these geological wonders and learn about the park's volcanic history. The park's namesake, Lassen Peak, is the largest plug dome volcano in the world and one of the most prominent features within the park. Visitors can hike to the summit of Lassen Peak for panoramic views of the surrounding landscape, including nearby volcanic peaks and alpine lakes.

: 2 to 2.5 hours west. San Francisco, California, is a vibrant and diverse city known for its iconic landmarks, rich history, cultural attractions, and stunning natural beauty. One of the most recognizable symbols of San Francisco, the Golden Gate Bridge spans the Golden Gate Strait, connecting the city to Marin County. Visitors can walk or bike across the bridge for breathtaking views of the San Francisco Bay and the city skyline. San Francisco's iconic cable cars are both a mode of transportation and a tourist attraction. Visitors can ride the historic cable cars along three routes, enjoying scenic views of the city's steep hills and landmarks such as Lombard Street, known as the "Crookedest Street in the World."
