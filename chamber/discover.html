History

Folsom's history is closely tied to the California Gold Rush. In 1848, gold was discovered in nearby Coloma, sparking the gold rush that brought thousands of fortune seekers to the region. Folsom's location along the American River made it an important hub for gold miners and prospectors.

Folsom was officially founded in 1855 and named after Joseph Libbey Folsom, who was one of the directors of the company that purchased the land where the town was established. Folsom quickly grew as a center of commerce and transportation due to its location on the Sacramento Valley Railroad.

One of the most well-known landmarks in Folsom is the Folsom State Prison, which was established in 1880. It gained notoriety for the song "Folsom Prison Blues" by Johnny Cash, who performed at the prison in 1968.

Folsom became one of the first cities in the United States to have a hydroelectric power plant in 1895. The Folsom Powerhouse, operated by the Sacramento Municipal Utility District (SMUD), played a crucial role in providing electricity to the region.

Folsom served as a vital transportation hub during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, thanks to its location along the Sacramento Valley Railroad. The railroad facilitated the transportation of goods and people to and from Folsom, contributing to its economic growth.

The Folsom Dam, completed in 1955, was constructed by the United States Army Corps of Engineers to control flooding along the American River. The reservoir created by the dam, Folsom Lake, is a popular recreational area today.

In recent decades, Folsom has experienced significant growth and development, becoming a thriving suburb of Sacramento. The city is known for its high quality of life, excellent schools, and recreational amenities.

Age Distribution: Folsom has a relatively young population, with a significant portion of residents falling into the age range of 25 to 44 years old. However, the city also has a sizable population of seniors aged 65 and older.

Ethnicity and Race: Folsom is a diverse community with residents from various ethnic and racial backgrounds. The population includes individuals of White, Hispanic or Latino, Asian, African American, and other ethnicities.

Education: Folsom is known for its highly educated population. Many residents hold bachelor's, master's, and doctoral degrees. The city is home to well-regarded schools and educational institutions.

Folsom is known for its excellent quality of life, with access to outdoor recreation, parks, trails, and cultural amenities. The city's proximity to Sacramento and the Sierra Nevada foothills makes it an attractive place to live for many.

Recreational Activities

Folsom is Distinctive by Nature. Enjoy more than 50 miles of paved recreational trails, Folsom Lake, Lake Natoma, and the serene American River. Mountatin biking, hiking, and paddleboarding are just a few of the popular activities of Folsom.

Folsom Lake State Recreation Area: Folsom Lake State Recreation Area is a popular destination for outdoor enthusiasts. Visitors can enjoy boating, fishing, swimming, hiking, picnicking, and camping along the shores of Folsom Lake. The park also features scenic trails for hiking, mountain biking, and horseback riding.

American River Parkway: The American River Parkway runs through Folsom and offers opportunities for walking, jogging, cycling, and horseback riding along the scenic American River. The parkway features paved trails, natural areas, and picnic areas with beautiful views of the river and surrounding landscape.

Historic Old Folsom: Historic Old Folsom is a charming district with historic buildings, shops, restaurants, and galleries. Visitors can stroll along Sutter Street, explore the Folsom History Museum, visit the Railroad Block, or take a guided walking tour to learn about the city's history.

Golf Courses: Folsom has several golf courses where enthusiasts can enjoy a round of golf surrounded by scenic landscapes. Courses such as Empire Ranch Golf Club and Folsom Lake Golf Course offer challenging holes and beautiful views of the surrounding area.

Surrounding Attractions

Take advantage of Folsom's relatively close proximity to various California attractions for a quick day trip and weekend get away.

  • Lake Tahoe: 75 miles to enjoy hiking, camping, snow skiing and various other outdoor activities.
  • Yosemite National Park: 150 miles southeast featuring granite cliffs, waterfalls, giant sequoia trees
  • Lassen Volcanic National Park: 160 miles northeast featuring steam vents, mud pots, hot springs, and a unique volcanic landscape.
  • San Francisco: 2 to 2.5 hours to scenic views of the Pacific Ocean, Golden Gate Bridge, Alcatraz Island, Fisherman's Wharf, and numerous other points of interest.
